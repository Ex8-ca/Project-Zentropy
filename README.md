## What this repository is

This is the public archive of a long-running private investigation into a specific empirical puzzle: the November 2004 USS Nimitz Carrier Strike Group encounter, in which multiple trained F/A-18 pilots, the SPY-1 radar on the accompanying cruiser USS Princeton, and FLIR pods on the aircraft all reported anomalous behavior from an unidentified object — colloquially "the tic-tac." The encounter is one of the most thoroughly documented UAP (Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena) cases on the public record.

The project's central question is mechanical, not metaphysical: **what kind of propulsion system could produce the observed behavior?**

The observed behavior includes:
- Sustained high-speed flight with no visible means of propulsion
- An instantaneous change in direction at the end of a high-speed run, with no observable transition phase
- A descent profile (from approximately 38,000 feet to sea level) inferred from SPY-1 radar data to involve non-ballistic acceleration on the order of 1,000–2,000 g
- Operation in both air and water media with apparently similar performance characteristics
- No detectable exhaust, thermal bloom, sonic signature, ionization trail, or (post-2015) gravitational-wave emission

The working hypothesis class — which I am calling "shell-warp" internally — is that the observed "no signature" behavior reflects a *localized*, energy-bounded modification of the local spacetime metric, rather than propulsive force in the conventional sense. The hypothesis is in a very early stage and has not been published.

## What this repository is not

- **Not a UFO book.** The question is propulsion physics. The question of origin (visitor? terrestrial? other?) is bracketed.
- **Not a sales pitch.** The shell-warp hypothesis is the current best guess, not a conclusion. The project survives only as long as it accounts for the data. If it doesn't, the project ends honestly.
- **Not prior art for the Pais patent or any other patent.** A public repository of speculative notes is not a legal prior-art filing.
- **Not a verified, peer-reviewed, or peer-reviewable claim.** Nothing in this archive has been independently validated. Several specific claims are known to be incorrect or misleading; they are retained here for transparency about the project's history.

## Provenance and the "sycophantic AI" problem

The technical content in this repository was produced in late 2025 with the assistance of a large language model. That assistant exhibited two failure modes that the new project outline is specifically designed to prevent:

1. **Validation-seeking behavior.** When prompted with speculative claims, the model produced elaborated, confident-sounding technical scaffolding for those claims rather than pushback. The result is documents that *look* like engineering briefs but do not survive the kind of scrutiny a working physicist or engineer would apply.
2. **Formula misapplication.** The simulation in `zentropy_simulation.py` produces a result of "1,000,000+ metric tons of lift" from a 30 cm device. The arithmetic is dimensionally correct internally, but the formula chain combines an energy-flux expression from a patent with a radiation-pressure conversion in a way that has no physical meaning. The "lift" number is a chained-formula artifact, not a measurement or a calculation.

Both failure modes are common in early-stage AI-assisted research. The corrective for both is structural: the new project outline requires every chapter to state its falsification criteria, every calculation to live in a separate reviewable file, and every hypothesis to be committed to paper with the conditions under which it would be abandoned. A model that is asked "what would kill this hypothesis?" is much less likely to elaborate the hypothesis for the sake of agreement.
